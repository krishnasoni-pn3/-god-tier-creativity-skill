# Creativity Evaluation Rubric

> Use this rubric to self-score creative output. If any dimension falls below 3, regenerate the concept using the Cognitive Scaffolding.

---

## Dimension 1: Novelty

**What it measures:** Does this concept create a genuinely new category, or is it a recombination of existing ideas?

| Score | Label | Anchor Example |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Already Exists** | "A food delivery app with faster delivery." This already exists — it's just DoorDash with a speed claim. |
| 2 | **Minor Twist** | "A food delivery app where you can also order groceries." Instacart + DoorDash. A combination, not an invention. |
| 3 | **Smart Recombination** | "A food delivery app where the chef comes to your home and cooks." Combines personal chef services with delivery convenience in a new way. |
| 4 | **Category Adjacent** | "A food experience where you join a stranger's dinner table tonight — a supper club matching algorithm." New category (stranger dining) using known patterns. |
| 5 | **Category Creator** | "Food arrives as raw ingredients with an AR ghost chef who appears in your kitchen and cooks alongside you in real-time." Nothing like this exists. It creates the category of "spectral cooking." |

**Common Failure Patterns:**
- Adding "but with AI" to an existing product (Score: 1-2)
- Combining two known apps without new emergent behavior (Score: 2)
- Describing a feature, not a concept (Score: 1-2)

**How to Improve 3→5:**
- Apply Step 3 (Cross-Pollination) more aggressively. Pick a more distant domain.
- Ask: "If this concept were explained to someone in 1920, would any part of it be comprehensible?" If yes, it's not novel enough.
- Invert another assumption you didn't think to invert.

## Dimension 2: Emotional Resonance

**What it measures:** Does this concept make someone *feel* something? Would they tell a friend about it without being asked?

| Score | Label | Anchor Example |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Informational** | "An analytics dashboard with better charts." Useful, but nobody tells their friends about charts. |
| 2 | **Mildly Interesting** | "A weather app that shows weather as paintings." Cute. You'd show someone once. Then forget. |
| 3 | **Genuinely Interesting** | "A weather app that plays a unique musical composition based on today's atmosphere — temperature becomes tempo, humidity becomes reverb." You'd probably tell one friend. |
| 4 | **Memorable** | "A weather app that generates a daily haiku from atmospheric data, and at the end of the year, they're compiled into a poetry book of your climate." You'd remember this. You'd want this. |
| 5 | **Unforgettable** | "An app that captures the exact color of the sky at the moment someone you love was born, and turns it into a physical paint swatch mailed to you on their birthday." You can't stop thinking about this. You're already imagining giving it to someone. |

**Common Failure Patterns:**
- Describing features instead of experiences (Score: 1-2)
- Being clever without being moving (Score: 3)
- Relying on novelty alone without human connection (Score: 2-3)

**How to Improve 3→5:**
- Connect to a universal human experience: love, loss, nostalgia, wonder, belonging.
- Add a physical or tangible element — digital-only concepts have lower emotional ceiling.
- Ask: "Would this make someone's eyes water, even slightly?" If not, go deeper.

## Dimension 3: Feasibility

**What it measures:** Could this actually be built or implemented with current or near-future technology?

> **Note:** The 🃏 Wildcard concept is exempt from this dimension. It's supposed to be borderline impossible.

| Score | Label | Anchor Example |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Violates Physics** | "A teleportation device for commuters." We cannot violate the laws of thermodynamics for your morning commute. |
| 2 | **Decades Away** | "A neural implant that lets you taste websites." Brain-computer interfaces aren't there yet. |
| 3 | **Possible with Major R&D** | "A fabric that changes color based on your heart rate." Technically possible with thermochromic materials, but non-trivial to manufacture at scale. |
| 4 | **Achievable with Effort** | "An app that generates a unique soundscape for each room based on its acoustic signature." All technology exists. Needs good engineering. |
| 5 | **Build It This Weekend** | "A website where you upload a photo of your bookshelf and it generates a personality profile based on your book collection." Camera + image recognition + book database + personality mapping. All exist now. |

**Common Failure Patterns:**
- Hand-waving over the hard engineering ("just use AI to...") (Score: 2-3)
- Confusing "I can describe it" with "it can be built" (Score: 1-2)
- Ignoring economics — technically possible but financially absurd (Score: 2)

**How to Improve 3→5:**
- Identify the single hardest technical challenge and name a specific approach to solving it.
- Reduce scope. The concept doesn't have to do everything — it has to do one thing brilliantly.
- Ask: "What's the MVP version of this that proves the core idea?"

## Dimension 4: Sensory Clarity

**What it measures:** Can the audience see, hear, feel, smell, or taste this concept from the description alone?

| Score | Label | Anchor Example |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Abstract** | "A platform for mindful productivity." What does this look like? Sound like? Feel like? Nobody knows. |
| 2 | **Vaguely Described** | "A calming app with soothing colors." Which colors? What's soothing? This could be anything. |
| 3 | **Some Vivid Details** | "An app with a deep indigo interface where tasks dissolve like watercolors when completed." Now I can see something. The "dissolve like watercolors" gives me a sensory hook. |
| 4 | **Rich Imagery** | "When you complete a task, the text softens, blurs, and bleeds downward like wet ink on handmade paper, pooling at the bottom of the screen into an abstract painting that grows throughout your day." I can see this clearly. I can imagine the animation. |
| 5 | **Full Sensory Immersion** | "The completed task dissolves like wet ink on washi paper, accompanied by the soft sound of a single ceramic wind chime. The ink pools at the bottom of your screen into an evolving watercolor — by evening, your day's work has become a painting. The background darkens gradually from cream to deep indigo as the day progresses, like a sky turning to dusk." I can see it, hear it, feel the passage of time. I'm inside this experience. |

**Common Failure Patterns:**
- Using adjectives instead of specifics ("beautiful, elegant, stunning") (Score: 1-2)
- Describing functionality without aesthetics (Score: 1)
- Only describing visuals, ignoring sound, touch, and time (Score: 3)

**How to Improve 3→5:**
- Add at least 2 sensory channels (visual + auditory, or visual + tactile).
- Describe what changes over time — static descriptions are less vivid than dynamic ones.
- Use similes from the physical world: "like wet ink," "like morning fog," "like a struck bell."

## Dimension 5: Naming Quality

**What it measures:** Does the name itself do creative work? Is it memorable, evocative, and self-revealing?

| Score | Label | Anchor Example |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Generic Label** | "TaskManager Pro" — tells you the category, nothing else. |
| 2 | **Descriptive** | "SmartTasks" — slightly better, but still just a feature description with "Smart" bolted on. |
| 3 | **Clever** | "Flowstate" — a play on "flow state" psychology. It's clever and relevant. But it's been done. |
| 4 | **Evocative** | "Undertow" — for a deep work tool. Evokes being pulled beneath the surface into focused depth. Creates a sensory image. |
| 5 | **Mythic** | "The Abyss Protocol" — for a deep work methodology. The name alone creates an entire mythology. You want to know what happens when you enter the Abyss. The name IS a story. |

**Common Failure Patterns:**
- Naming the category instead of the experience (Score: 1)
- Being clever without being evocative (Score: 2-3)
- Using overused "beautiful" words (Nova, Aura, Zenith) (Score: 2)

**How to Improve 3→5:**
- Use the naming frameworks in [naming_patterns.md](naming_patterns.md).
- Ask: "Does this name make someone ask 'What's that?'" If yes, it's at least a 4.
- Say the name aloud. Does it feel good in the mouth? Sound has power.

## Composite Scoring

Different use cases should weigh dimensions differently:

| Use Case | Novelty | Emotional Resonance | Feasibility | Sensory Clarity | Naming |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Product Design** | 30% | 20% | 30% | 10% | 10% |
| **Brand & Marketing** | 15% | 30% | 10% | 15% | 30% |
| **Narrative / World-Building** | 25% | 35% | 5% | 30% | 5% |
| **UX / Digital Experience** | 20% | 25% | 25% | 25% | 5% |
| **Pure Ideation / Brainstorm** | 35% | 25% | 10% | 20% | 10% |

## The God-Tier Test

A concept achieves **God-Tier** status when it scores:
- **5 in Novelty** — it creates a new category
- **5 in Emotional Resonance** — it's unforgettable
- **AND at least one other dimension at 5**

God-Tier concepts are rare. If you generate one per session, you're performing at the highest level. When you find one, mark it with: ⚡ **GOD-TIER** ⚡
