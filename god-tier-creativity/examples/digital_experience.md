# Worked Example: Digital Experience Design

> These examples demonstrate the full 5-step Cognitive Scaffolding applied to digital product and experience prompts.

---

## Example 1: "Invent a new form of digital journaling"

### 🧠 Step 1: Deconstruction (The Baseline)

Expected answer: A notes app with a calendar view, writing prompts, mood tracking, maybe AI-generated reflections. Looks like Day One, Notion, or Journey. Text-based, chronological, private. **Every journaling app since 2010.**

Core assumptions:
- Journaling is writing text
- Entries are organized chronologically (a timeline)
- Journaling is private and solitary
- Older entries are "in the past" — archived and forgotten
- The journal is a record of what happened

### 🧠 Step 2: Inversion (The Void)

| Assumption | Inversion |
|---|---|
| Journaling is writing text | What if it had no text at all? |
| Entries are chronological | What if time was vertical, not horizontal — layers, not a timeline? |
| Old entries are archived | What if old entries actively changed over time? |
| A journal records what happened | What if it recorded what you *felt*, not what you did? |

### 🧠 Step 3: Cross-Pollination (The Catalyst)

**Domain: Geology** — the science of Earth's structure, focusing on sedimentation, stratification, erosion, fossil formation, and deep time.

Key principles:
- Sedimentary layers stack over deep time — newer covers older, but older shapes everything above
- Fossils are compressed imprints of living things, preserved in stone
- Erosion removes surface layers, revealing ancient strata beneath
- Metamorphism — extreme pressure transforms one type of rock into another

### 🧠 Step 4: Synthesis (The Masterpiece)

---

**Concept: *Stratum***

**The Core Idea:** A journaling app where every entry is a geological layer, not a page. Your journal isn't a book — it's a cross-section of earth. You dig down through your own history.

**Why It's Different:** Traditional journals are flat timelines. Stratum is *vertical*. Today's entry sits on top of yesterday's, which sits on top of last month's, which sits on top of last year's. Over time, your journal becomes a living geological column — and the deeper you dig, the more compressed, transformed, and fossilized your past entries become.

**The Experience:** You open Stratum and see a cross-section of layered earth — rich, textured, colored differently based on your emotional state when you wrote each entry. Today's layer is soft, pliable, fresh sediment. You tap it and it opens for editing — a freeform canvas where you deposit words, photos, voice recordings, sketches. They land like pebbles and shells falling into wet sand.

As days pass, today's layer compresses under the weight of new entries above. After a week, it's dense siltstone. After a month, it's hard sandstone — you can still read it, but the edges have softened, details compressed. After a year, it's metamorphic rock — the entry has been *transformed*. The exact words may be gone, but the emotional fossil remains: a compressed imprint of how you felt, rendered as abstract geological art.

Swiping down through the column, you see years of your life as geological strata — bands of color, texture, and density. Dark bands of grief. Bright, crystalline veins of joy. You can literally *see* the shape of your life in cross-section.

**Implementation Hooks:**
- **Erosion Events:** Periodically, Stratum runs "erosion" — it removes surface details from old entries (timestamps, minor edits) while preserving the core emotional signature. This isn't data loss — it's intentional fossilization.
- **Fossil View:** Deep entries (6+ months) are rendered as "fossils" — abstract, compressed visual imprints that capture the *feeling* of the entry without the literal content. Generated using sentiment analysis + generative art.
- **Core Sample:** A feature that lets you drill a vertical "core sample" through all layers at a specific date each year — see every January 15th of your life stacked on top of each other. How have your Januaries changed?

---

### 🃏 Wildcard: *The Fault Line*

What if the journal tracked the "tectonic" pressures in your life — work, relationships, health, creativity — as continental plates? When two plates push against each other (work-life conflict), a "fault line" appears in your journal's geology. If the pressure isn't released, you get an "earthquake" — a dramatic visual rupture in your journal's strata, accompanied by a notification: "Your work and health plates have been grinding for 3 weeks. The pressure is building. What will you release?" The journal doesn't just record your life — it *predicts your breakdowns* using geological metaphors.

## Example 2: "Design a music discovery experience"

### 🧠 Step 1: Deconstruction (The Baseline)

Expected answer: Algorithmic recommendations, "Discover Weekly" playlist, genre-based browsing, collaborative playlists, AI DJ. Every streaming platform already does this. **Spotify's Discover Weekly was innovative in 2015. It's now table stakes.**

Core assumptions:
- Discovery means the algorithm shows you songs you'll like
- Music is organized by genre, artist, or mood
- Discovery is passive — you listen to what's served
- You discover one song at a time
- Discovery is solitary (your personal taste profile)

### 🧠 Step 2: Inversion (The Void)

| Assumption | Inversion |
|---|---|
| Algorithm serves you music | What if you had to *forage* for music yourself? |
| Music organized by genre | What if music was organized by underground connection, not category? |
| Discovery is passive | What if discovery required effort — and the effort was the point? |
| Discovery is solitary | What if you discovered music through a living, growing network? |

### 🧠 Step 3: Cross-Pollination (The Catalyst)

**Domain: Mycology** — the study of fungi, focusing on mycelium networks, spore dispersal, symbiotic relationships, and decomposition.

Key principles:
- Mycelium is an underground network connecting trees in a forest — the "Wood Wide Web." Trees share nutrients through fungal connections.
- Spores travel on wind and water, landing randomly, growing only where conditions are right
- Symbiosis — fungi and trees sustain each other; neither thrives alone
- Decomposition — fungi break down dead matter to feed new growth

### 🧠 Step 4: Synthesis (The Masterpiece)

---

**Concept: *Mycelium***

**The Core Idea:** A music discovery platform where songs aren't recommended by algorithms — they *spread* through an underground network of human connections, like nutrients flowing through fungal mycelium.

**Why It's Different:** Spotify treats you as an isolated taste profile fed by a recommendation engine. Mycelium treats the entire listening community as a forest — and the music flows between people through underground connections that neither you nor the platform fully controls.

**The Experience:** When you join Mycelium, you plant a "tree" — your musical identity, rooted in 10 songs you love. Your tree joins the forest of all other users' trees. Beneath the surface, invisible mycelial threads begin connecting your tree to others based on *unexpected* overlaps — not "you both like pop" but "you both listen to this obscure Icelandic band AND this 1970s Bollywood soundtrack AND this field recording of rain in Kyoto."

When another tree in your network discovers a new song and plays it repeatedly, that song's *nutrients* begin flowing through the mycelium toward your tree. It appears in your "root system" — a visual underground view where you see glowing particles flowing toward you from unknown sources. You don't know WHO sent it. You don't know WHY the network connected you. You just know: the forest thinks you need this song.

If you love the song, your root system glows brighter and sends it deeper. If you skip it, it decomposes — breaking down into its musical components (tempo, key, instrumentation) which feed the network's understanding of your taste at a molecular level.

**Implementation Hooks:**
- **The Forest Floor:** Your home screen is a bird's-eye view of a bioluminescent forest floor, pulsing with flowing particles of music. Tapping a glow reveals the song. Zooming out shows the entire forest — millions of trees interconnected.
- **Spore Mode:** When you find a song you're obsessed with, you can "sporify" it — release it as a spore into the wind. It'll land on random trees across the forest. If it grows there, you've created a new connection. If it doesn't, it decomposes and feeds the soil.
- **Symbiosis Scores:** After 6 months, the network reveals your deepest symbiotic connections — the people whose musical taste most consistently enriches yours. You never chose each other. The mycelium chose for you.

---

### 🃏 Wildcard: *The Decomposer*

A feature that takes songs you used to love but now skip — your "dead musical matter" — and breaks them down into their atomic components: BPM, key, harmonic structure, lyrical themes, production style. It then uses those decomposed nutrients to grow *entirely new music* — AI-generated songs that contain the DNA of your past loves, recombined into something you've never heard. Your dead songs literally feed the birth of new ones. Music composting.
