# Anti-Patterns: The Creativity Blacklist

> If you catch yourself generating any of these, STOP. Re-run the Cognitive Scaffolding. These are the dead ideas that pass for creativity.

---

## 1. Technology & Product Clichés

| 🚫 Cliché | Why It Fails | ✅ Do This Instead |
|---|---|---|
| "AI-powered [anything]" | It's a technology label, not an idea. Everything will be AI-powered. This differentiates nothing. | Describe the *behavior* that emerges. "The tool learns your hesitation patterns" is 100x better than "AI-powered writing assistant." |
| "A platform that connects..." | Every startup pitch since 2010. LinkedIn connects professionals. Tinder connects singles. You're not adding anything. | Define the *physics* of the connection. What force? What friction? What happens at the moment of contact? |
| "Uber for X" | Analogy-as-innovation is not innovation. It signals you can't describe what makes your thing unique. | Describe what's new without referencing another company. If you can't, you don't have an idea yet. |
| "Blockchain-based / Web3" | Technology-first thinking. Most problems don't need a blockchain. It's a hammer looking for nails. | Start with the human problem. What trust deficit exists? Solve that — the technology is implementation detail. |
| "Gamification with points and badges" | Extrinsic motivation. People game the system, then leave when bored. Duolingo's owl is not a business model. | Design *intrinsic* motivation. Why would someone do this without any reward? Make the activity itself compelling. |
| "Disrupting the [industry]" | The word "disrupt" has been disrupted. It now means nothing. It's the "synergy" of the 2020s. | Describe the *specific mechanism* of change. How does the old way break? What exact pain point vanishes? |
| "One-stop shop / all-in-one" | Feature bloat disguised as value proposition. Being everything to everyone means being nothing to anyone. | Be the best at exactly one thing. Name that thing. Describe why being the best at it matters more than breadth. |
| "Seamless experience" | Every product claims this. None achieve it. It's become meaningless marketing filler. | Describe the specific *seam* you're removing. "You never have to re-enter your address" beats "seamless experience." |
| "Democratizing X" | Overused by every SaaS company trying to sound noble. Usually means "making it cheaper." | Be honest about the value. If you're making it cheaper, say that. If you're making it accessible, describe the specific barrier you're removing. |
| "Social impact" / "Making the world better" | Vague morality signaling. How? For whom? At what cost? | Be brutally specific. "Reduces water usage by 40% in textile manufacturing for factories under 50 employees in Southeast Asia." |
| "Frictionless" | Close cousin of "seamless." Equally empty. | Name the friction. Describe the exact moment of frustration. Then show how it vanishes. |
| "End-to-end solution" | Enterprise jargon for "we do everything, badly." | Pick a start point and an end point. Describe the journey between them in human terms. |
| "Ecosystem" (for anything not actually an ecosystem) | A login page and two integrations is not an ecosystem. | If you have fewer than 5 independent, interconnected components, it's a product. Call it a product. |
| "Leveraging data" | Data is not a verb. This says nothing about what you actually do with it. | Describe the specific insight that emerges from the specific data. "We noticed that 73% of cancellations happen on Tuesdays" is a story. "We leverage data" is nothing. |
| "Real-time collaboration" | Google Docs did this in 2006. It's table stakes, not a differentiator. | Describe *what kind* of collaboration and *why it matters for your specific use case.* |

## 2. Design & Aesthetic Clichés

| 🚫 Cliché | Why It Fails | ✅ Do This Instead |
|---|---|---|
| "Sleek, minimalist design" | It's the default aesthetic of 2015-2025. It signals "I didn't think about design, so I chose the safe option." | Invent a new aesthetic vocabulary. Name it. "Moss Brutalism" — rough concrete textures with living plant integrations. |
| "Blue for trust, green for growth" | Pop-psychology color theory from a 2012 infographic. Every bank is blue. Every health app is green. | Use color *wrong* on purpose. A funeral home in bright yellow. A children's toy in matte black. Justify the unexpected choice. |
| "Hero section with stock photo" | A smiling person in front of a laptop on a gradient background. The visual equivalent of saying nothing. | Use illustration, animation, or real photography. If you must use a hero section, put something surprising in it. |
| "Gradient everything" | Gradients were fresh in 2018. Now they're the new flat design — everywhere, undifferentiated. | Use a single, bold, flat color as your primary identity. Or use texture instead of gradients. |
| "Dark mode as personality" | Dark mode is a feature, not a design identity. Having a dark mode doesn't make your product "premium." | Define what "premium" means for your product through interaction quality, not color scheme. |
| "Rounded corners on everything" | Bubbly, friendly, approachable — and identical to every other product. | Mix geometric styles. Use sharp corners where tension is appropriate, soft where comfort is needed. |
| "Glassmorphism / frosted glass" | Was novel in 2021. Now it's the equivalent of skeuomorphic leather textures circa 2010. | Invent a new visual metaphor. What does "erosion-morphism" look like? What about "moss-morphism"? |
| "Card-based layout" | Pinterest, Trello, and every dashboard since 2014. Cards are useful but not creative. | What if content wasn't in cards? What if it was in pools, or orbits, or geological strata? |
| "Hamburger menu" | Hides navigation behind a mystery icon. Users don't find what they can't see. | Make navigation visible, or invent a new navigation metaphor entirely. |
| "Infinite scroll" | Dopamine exploitation disguised as UX. The design equivalent of a casino with no clocks. | Give content a natural ending. Let users feel completion. Design for satisfaction, not addiction. |

## 3. Naming & Branding Clichés

| 🚫 Cliché | Why It Fails | ✅ Do This Instead |
|---|---|---|
| Removing vowels (Flickr, Tumblr) | A 2005-2010 pattern. It now signals "we couldn't get the domain with vowels." | Use the full word. Or invent a new one entirely. |
| Adding "-ly" or "-ify" (Shopify, Grammarly) | Pattern exhausted. There are hundreds of -ly/-ify names. You're blending into a crowd. | Use a naming framework from [naming_patterns.md](naming_patterns.md). Try Metaphor Transfer or Compound Myth. |
| Generic Latin/Greek roots (Verizon, Accenture) | Sounds "corporate" but means nothing. Nobody knows or cares about the etymology. | Use a word that creates a mental image. "Obsidian" beats "Veridix" every time. |
| Meaningless portmanteaus (Pinterest = Pin + Interest) | Clever for one second, then forgotten. The portmanteau rarely captures what the product actually is. | If combining words, make the combination create a third meaning. "Thunderbird" = thunder + bird = mythic power. |
| Adding ".io" / ".ai" to domain | These TLDs are now so common they've lost all signaling value. | Get the .com. Or use a creative TLD that adds meaning (.studio, .design, .earth). |
| Names that are just verbs + nouns (TaskRabbit, DoorDash) | Functional but forgettable. Describes what you do, not what you *are*. | Name the feeling, not the function. What emotion does your service create? |
| Lowercase tech aesthetic (airbnb, spotify) | Once distinctive, now default. The lowercase style says "I'm a tech company" and nothing else. | Consider uppercase, title case, or mixed case. Or make the typography itself part of the brand identity. |
| Abstract placeholder names (Aura, Nova, Zenith) | Beautiful words that have been used by 10,000 companies. They mean nothing now. | Make the name specific to YOUR story. A real word that's unusual is better than a pretty word that's overused. |
| Acronyms (AWS, IBM, SAP) | Only works if you're already a mega-corporation. For startups, it's an obstacle to memory. | Use a full, memorable word. Nobody remembers your four-letter acronym. |
| "_hub" or "_space" suffix (GitHub, MySpace) | Signals a platform, but it's the generic platform suffix. Like naming a restaurant "FoodPlace." | Name the *culture* of the platform, not the container. |

## 4. Narrative & Storytelling Clichés

| 🚫 Cliché | Why It Fails | ✅ Do This Instead |
|---|---|---|
| The Chosen One | Disempowers the audience. Only one special person can save the world. Boring since 1977. | Make the hero ordinary. Their ordinariness IS the point. Anyone could be them. |
| Love triangle | Reduces complex relationships to a geometry problem. Two options isn't drama — it's a binary choice. | Design a relationship web. Five people, all connected differently. That's where real tension lives. |
| "It was all a dream" | Invalidates everything the audience just experienced. It's a betrayal of trust disguised as a twist. | Make the twist recontextualize everything — not erase it. The audience should want to re-read, not throw the book. |
| Dystopian YA (authoritarian government + teen rebel) | Hunger Games did it. Divergent did it. The genre is exhausted. | What if the rebellion was wrong? What if the authoritarian government was actually correct? Moral complexity beats simplicity. |
| Dead parents as motivation | Lazy backstory. Batman, Harry Potter, and 10,000 others. It's emotional manipulation, not character development. | Give the protagonist living, complicated parents who are part of the problem. That's harder and better. |
| Prophecy that must be fulfilled | Removes agency from the characters. If fate decided everything, why are we watching? | Let characters make choices that create the "prophecy" retroactively — agency disguised as destiny. |
| Villain monologue | No real villain explains their plan to the hero. It exists only to give the audience exposition. | Let the audience discover the plan through consequences, not explanation. Show, don't monologue. |
| Amnesia as a plot device | Convenient way to withhold information. The audience knows it's a trick. | Give the character perfect memory but inability to interpret it. They remember everything but understand nothing. |
| Training montage | Compresses the hardest, most interesting part of the story into 90 seconds. | Make the training the story. Show the failures, the plateaus, the moments of doubt. That's where the drama is. |
| Magical minority (wise mentor from marginalized group) | Reduces a complex identity to a narrative function. The minority character exists only to help the white protagonist. | Give every character their own story, desires, and agency independent of the protagonist. |

## 5. Pitch & Presentation Clichés

| 🚫 Cliché | Why It Fails | ✅ Do This Instead |
|---|---|---|
| "Imagine a world where..." | Every TED talk opener since 2010. The audience has heard it 10,000 times. | Start with a specific, vivid, surprising fact. "Last Tuesday, a fish taught itself to walk." |
| "What if I told you..." | Neo called. He wants his pill metaphor back. It's condescending. | Just tell them. Confidence doesn't need a conditional opener. |
| Hockey stick growth projections | Every startup claims exponential growth. None of them show the flat line that comes first. | Show the honest curve. Include the "trough of sorrow." Honesty is more compelling than fantasy. |
| "We're not just building X, we're building Y" | Sounds deep, actually says nothing. "We're not just building a toaster, we're building the future of breakfast." | Pick one thing. Say it with conviction. Simplicity is confidence. |
| "Our team is our greatest asset" | A slide with headshots and LinkedIn titles. Nobody cares. | Tell a story about what your team *did*, not who they are. "Our CTO built a compiler in a weekend" beats "Stanford PhD." |
| TAM/SAM/SOM slide with a huge market size | "$4.7 trillion market" — and you're going to capture 0.00001% of it. This impresses nobody. | Show how you capture ONE customer deeply. The market size is irrelevant if you can't explain one sale. |
| "We have no competition" | Either you're lying or your market doesn't exist. Both are bad. | Name your competitors, then explain the specific advantage they can't replicate. |
| Steve Jobs quotes | He's been quoted so much that his words have lost all meaning. | Quote someone unexpected. A marine biologist. A jazz musician. A medieval blacksmith. |
| "Industry X is broken" | Every pitch deck slide 2. If the industry were truly broken, it wouldn't exist. | Be specific about WHAT is broken, for WHOM, and WHY it hasn't been fixed yet. |
| Mission statement slide | A wall of text that took weeks to craft and nobody will remember. | Reduce your mission to 5 words or fewer. If you can't, you don't understand your mission yet. |

## 6. Tone & Language Clichés

| 🚫 Cliché | Why It Fails | ✅ Do This Instead |
|---|---|---|
| "Leveraging synergies" | Peak corporate emptiness. It means "using things together" but sounds like a robot having a stroke. | Say what you mean in plain language. "We combined the sales and marketing teams." |
| "At the end of the day" | Filler phrase that adds zero meaning. It's verbal furniture. | Delete it. The sentence is stronger without it. |
| "Move the needle" | Which needle? By how much? In which direction? It's specificity-avoidant. | Use numbers. "Increase conversion by 12%" beats "move the needle" every time. |
| "Low-hanging fruit" | Implies you're too lazy to climb. It frames ambition as optional. | "We'll start with the highest-impact change that takes the least effort." Longer, but actually means something. |
| "Think outside the box" | The most inside-the-box phrase about thinking outside the box. The irony is terminal. | Just... think outside the box. Don't announce it. Do it. |
| "Paradigm shift" | Thomas Kuhn is rolling in his grave. This phrase has shifted so many paradigms it's meaningless. | Describe the specific before-and-after. "Before: people rented DVDs. After: they stream everything." |
| "Best-in-class" | Self-proclaimed excellence is not excellence. Nobody believes you. | Show evidence. A specific metric. A customer quote. An award. |
| "Innovative" / "Cutting-edge" | If you have to say you're innovative, you probably aren't. Innovation is visible — it doesn't need a label. | Show the innovation. Describe what's new. Let the audience conclude "that's innovative" themselves. |
| "Circle back" / "Touch base" | Meeting-culture jargon that replaces commitment with vagueness. | "I'll send you the analysis by Thursday at 3pm." Specificity is respect. |
| "Deep dive" | Overused to the point where a "deep dive" is now a shallow paddle. | Describe the depth. "We'll examine each line of the financial model" beats "we'll do a deep dive." |
| "Robust" / "Scalable" / "Flexible" | The holy trinity of saying nothing. Every product claims all three. | Describe the specific scenario. "Handles 10 million concurrent users without degradation" beats "scalable." |
